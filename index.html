Hebdomad is a Xenharmonic Musical Instrument

You play Hebdomad on the home row of your keyboard where asdf and jkl; are notes, and g and h control the volume.

Xenharmonic What?

In Western music, we use a tuning that divides an octave into twelve equal parts. This tuning is called 12-tone Equal Temperament. Hebdomad is an instrument for exploring the music of the spaces inbetween these twelve tones. There are sonic mysteries old and new hidden here, the world of Xenharmonic music!

Hebdomad is built for heptatonic scales, which are basically just scales with seven notes. You can view the scale degrees by clicking the Deg button in the Controls tab. They are numbered from 0 to 7.

Controls

Hebdomad uses basic sound waves to produce its voice. These sound waves are shaped and sculpted in the Controls tab.

Select a base waveform for Hebdomad from the Oscillator section. The sine wave is the most pure and is great for hearing a new tuning. The other waveforms are richer and are best when shaped with the Amplitude Envelope and Low-Pass Filter.

The Amplitude Envelope shapes the body and character of the voice. Basically, an amplitude envelope controls how loud a note is when you press a key, after you let go of the key, and the time inbetween. You can think of Attack, Decay, Sustain and Release as phases in the journey of a note. Each of them are measured in milliseconds.

Attack defines how quickly the sound grows, Sustain defines the volume of the held note, and Release how quickly the sound fades away when you release the key. Decay allows the note to get loud at the end of the attack phase, then drop down to the sustain level.

The Low-pass Filter (LPF) removes some of the higher frequencies from a sound. This doesn't matter much with a Sine wave, but for the other waveforms it can soften the sound and give it a different character.

The Amp controls the volume of the instrument. You can also control the volume with g and h on your keyboard.

The Keyboard Settings change the labels on the keyboard: Key for keyboard characters, Deg for scale degrees, and Cent display the current tuning for each key.

Retune

The Retune tab gives you a number of tunings to experiment with. There are two main categories of tunings available: Trivalent Just and Equal Temperaments. Just click on any option, and Hebdomad updates to the new tuning automatically.

Each tuning you select gives you a breakdown of its steps represented in cents. Cents are based on our 12-tone Equal Temperament system. Think of each step between the twelve tones (a "half-step") as a dollar. Each of these is worth 100 cents, but we can take more or less cents to create all sorts of new tunings!

Trivalent Just tunings use whole number ratios to create range of beautiful and rich scales. The theory behind these tunings is fascinating, but it's far to much to get into here. A good starting point for exploring these tunings is the 5/4, 3/2 Lydian scale or the 13/11, 3/2 Dorian scale.

Equal Temperaments are the scales you are probably most used to. Here you can find Major, Minor and Harmonic Minor scales. You can also find a 7-tone equal temperament which sounds really different!

Custom

In the Custom tab you can set a Base Frequency, which is basically sets where your scale starts. Choose a lower or higher frequency to change the range of your scale.

The row of boxes below Base Frequency are for defining a custom tuning. Each box corresponds to a key on Hebdomad's keyboard. One fun experiment is to set each box 50 cents apart: 0-50-100-150-200-250-300-350. This is a quarter-tone scale, which is fun to play quickly up and down the scale.
